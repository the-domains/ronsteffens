Seeking hope in challenging times.

Our current era of environmental communication is undergoing a tidal surge, it seems, of contradictory motivations and attitudes. As a professor of environmental communication and an editor and writer focused on wildfire, global climate change, land management, and rural/urban environmental issues, I find my browsing and research often lands amid pessimism (and its evil twin, inaction) and the essential need for giving a civil voice (and often civil disobedience) to a deep-seated reverence for a sustainable landscape, human and natural.

Seeking some middle ground amid balance and insight, I offer this occasional list of articles, opinions and references to help us understand and engage in the ongoing changes in audience and the environment, which may range from elevated advocacy to alienation to a potential radical-right swing to reactionary land management policies and energy boosterism.

This week’s links — the first post-election summary — offers a look back at Trump’s promises (threats?) as a candidate and how the focused world of environmental journalists and experts are starting to synthesis the risk of embedding anti-environmental rhetoricians in the White House.

Prospects for the Climate, and Environmentalism, Under President Trump

President Donald J. Trump. Get used to the sound of that, my environment-oriented friends. Is this end times for environmental progress or, more specifically, climate progress? No. The bad news about climate change is, in a way, the good news: The main forces determining emission levels of heat-trapping carbon dioxide will be just as much out of President Trump's hands as they were out of President Obama's.

First day in Malawi

It's good to return to the warm heart of Africa with three colleagues, here to teach wildfire management to Malawians in the south (in Liwonde, for week one) and center-north (based out of Luwawa for weeks two and three).
Tonight, at Heuglin's Lodge in Lilongwe, we continued our planning over a good meal (pork, squash, a wonderful pear desert) and talked with another guest about her work here in reducing HIV risk for teenagers.
Tonight I sleep under mosquito netting and the dogs bark till the full night and sleep under a filling moon.
Tomorrow we drive to Liwonde and the Hippo View Lodge. And Monday we start teaching, and of course learning too.
